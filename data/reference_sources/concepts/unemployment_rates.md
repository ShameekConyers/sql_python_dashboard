# Unemployment Rates: U-3 and U-6

The Bureau of Labor Statistics publishes multiple measures of labor underutilization. The two tracked in this dashboard are U-3 (the headline unemployment rate, FRED series UNRATE) and U-6 (the broadest measure, FRED series U6RATE).

## U-3: The Official Unemployment Rate

U-3 counts people who are jobless, have actively looked for work in the past four weeks, and are currently available for work, divided by the civilian labor force. It is the rate quoted in news headlines and is seasonally adjusted by the BLS.

U-3 does not capture workers who have stopped looking for a job (discouraged workers) or those working part-time because they cannot find full-time employment. This undercounting is why economists also track U-6.

## U-6: The Broadest Measure

U-6 adds to U-3 all marginally attached workers (people who want a job and looked in the past year but not the past four weeks) plus workers employed part-time for economic reasons. U-6 is always higher than U-3; the gap between the two measures reveals how much slack exists in the labor market beyond the headline number.

A widening U-6 minus U-3 gap signals that more workers face involuntary part-time hours or have dropped out of the active job search, which often occurs in early stages of a downturn before U-3 itself rises significantly.

## BLS Methodology

Both rates come from the Current Population Survey (CPS), a monthly household survey of approximately 60,000 households conducted by the Census Bureau on behalf of the BLS. Seasonal adjustment removes predictable calendar effects like holiday hiring. The reference period is the week containing the 12th of each month.

## Labor Force Participation

Neither U-3 nor U-6 captures long-term demographic shifts in labor force participation, such as aging baby boomers exiting the workforce or changing school enrollment patterns. Changes in participation can push U-3 down even without new job creation if people leave the labor force entirely.
